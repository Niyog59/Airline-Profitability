About the Dataset

  • Source: Kaggle, Airline Route Profitability Dataset by Muhammad Waleed
  • Context: Simulated full-year financial data for a major Middle Eastern airline based in Dubai (DXB)
  • Size: 7,974 flights across multiple routes, seasons, and aircraft types
  • Goal: Find what actually drives airline profitability
  • Key metrics: We use both Profit and Profit Margin, since raw profit alone can be misleading

Route Network and Fleet

  • Short-Haul (1 to 3.5 hrs): DOH, MCT, KWI, BAH, RUH, JED, CAI, AMM
  • Medium-Haul (2.5 to 7 hrs): BOM, DEL, IST, LHR, BLR, HYD, MAA, KHI, LHE, CMB
  • Long-Haul (6.5 to 16.5 hrs): JFK, SYD, MEL, SIN, HKG, BKK, KUL, CDG, FRA, LAX, SFO, ORD
  • Aircraft types: Boeing 737-800, Airbus A320, Boeing 777-300ER, Airbus A380, Boeing 787-9, Airbus A350-900

A Dubai-based carrier operates both regional and intercontinental flights, so profitability depends on both route scale and operating efficiency.

Key Variables in the Analysis

  • Profit: Total earnings after costs
  • Profit_Margin: Profit as a percentage of revenue
  • Load_Factor: How full the flight was
  • Route_Category: Short-haul, medium-haul, or long-haul
  • Season: Peak, Shoulder, Normal, or Low
  • Demand_Level: High or Medium demand
  • Flight_Date: Used for monthly trend analysis

These variables help compare both how much money flights make and how efficiently they make it.

Correct Season Definitions

  • Peak: January to February and December
  • Shoulder: March to April and October to November
  • Normal: May and September
  • Low: June to August

This is important because it means winter months are Peak and summer months are Low, which explains the monthly profit trend later.

How Does Profit Spread Across Flights?

Most flights earn between $0 and $500K. Some operate at a loss, while a smaller number earn much more than the rest.

Are Long-Haul Routes More Profitable?

Long-haul routes generally earn the highest total profit, while short-haul routes perform the weakest.

Are Long-Haul Routes More Efficient?

Long-haul routes appear to be the most efficient overall because they have the highest typical profit margins. However, there is still overlap, so this is a general pattern, not a rule for every single flight.

Does Season Affect Profit?

The pattern is clear: Peak performs best, followed by Shoulder, then Normal, with Low performing worst.

Demand Level is the Strongest Driver

This is the clearest pattern in the dataset. High-demand flights are much more profitable than medium-demand flights.

Demand Level Also Improves Efficiency

Demand level gives an even cleaner separation than season, which is why it is the strongest overall driver.

Fuller Flights = More Profit

All flights already have a high load factor, so the data looks compact and clustered. Even then, a lot of flights still fall below break-even. So fuller flights help, but costs still matter.

Profit Over Time

Winter months perform best because they fall into Peak season. Summer months perform worst because they fall into Low season.

Key Takeaways

  • Demand level is the strongest predictor of profitability
  • Long-haul routes earn the most total profit, but are not always the most efficient
  • Season clearly matters once the correct definitions are used. Peak = winter, Low = summer
  • Load factor helps, but it does not guarantee profit on its own
  • Best overall pattern: Peak season + Long Haul

Limitations and Future Work

Limitations:

  • This dataset is simulated, not taken directly from a real airline
  • It reflects one airline setting based in Dubai
  • Results may not generalize to all airlines or regions

Future work could explore:

  • Profitability by aircraft type
  • Profitability by specific destination
  • Predictive modeling for flight profit
